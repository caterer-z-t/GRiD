Paper¶

Summary¶

Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)] is a causal, independent, and highly heritable (\(\text{h}^2 >70\%\)) cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk factor that is elevated in an estimated 1.5 billion people globally [1, 2, 3]. Elevated Lp(a) is a genetically established risk factor for coronary artery disease, myocardial infarction, aortic valve stenosis, and stroke [4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9]. Plasma Lp(a) concentrations are predominantly determined by copy number variation (CNV) in the Kringle IV type-2 (KIV-2) variable number tandem repeat (VNTR) within the LPA gene [10]. The KIV-2 repeat unit spans approximately 5.5 kb and individuals carry 1–40 copies per haplotype, making the total VNTR region one of the largest and most structurally complex in the human genome and rendering KIV-2 CNV the single most important genetic determinant of Lp(a) levels [2, 3, 11]. Critically, KIV-2 copy number is inversely correlated with Lp(a) concentration: individuals with fewer copies tend to have higher Lp(a) and elevated CVD risk [4, 5, 10]. Accurately measuring KIV-2 CNV from whole-genome sequencing (WGS) is central to building accurate cross-population Lp(a) polygenic risk scores (PRS) and to enabling the causal inference studies needed to characterize Lp(a)’s phenotypic effects and inform therapeutic strategies across diverse populations [1, 11].

GRiD (Genomic Repeat inference from Depth) is an open-source Python pipeline that addresses this measurement gap. GRiD estimates diploid and haplotype-resolved VNTR copy number from short-read WGS data by combining read-depth normalization, read depth profile-matched nearest-neighbor estimation, and identity-by-descent (IBD)-based haplotype inference [12]. While GRiD was developed and validated for the LPA KIV-2 locus its core read-depth normalization and neighbor-based copy number framework is locus-agnostic and applicable to any large VNTR. The pipeline supports multi-threading and is driven by a single YAML configuration file, making it accessible to researchers without extensive bioinformatics expertise.

Statement of Need¶

Accurate haplotype-resolved KIV-2 copy number estimation is a prerequisite for understanding the full genetic architecture of Lp(a) variation. Although several tools have recently addressed parts of this problem, none provides the complete workflow (standard short-read WGS to haplotype-resolved copy number estimates) in an open-source, cohort-scalable, and well-documented form. KILDA [13] estimates diploid KIV-2 copy number directly from FASTQ files using an alignment-free k-mer approach, but does not resolve haplotype-specific copy numbers. The Illumina DRAGEN LPA Caller offers KIV-2 estimation on the proprietary DRAGEN platform but is not open-source and requires specific hardware. Kivvi [14] enables KIV-2 copy number estimation from PacBio HiFi long-read data, but long-read WGS remains unavailable for the large population cohorts where most epidemiological Lp(a) research is currently conducted. vntr-calling-nf [15] provides a Nextflow-based signature-sequence pipeline for VNTR variant calling but targets coding variant detection rather than copy number quantification, and has been primarily validated on whole-exome sequencing data. Researchers studying Lp(a) at cohort scale with short-read WGS have therefore continued to rely on convoluted, opaque, piecemeal, ad hoc scripts or unpublished internal pipelines, creating barriers to reproducibility and cross-cohort comparability. GRiD addresses this gap by providing a single, installable, documented tool that encapsulates the entire estimation workflow in a reproducible, multi-threaded, and configurable pipeline.

The LPA KIV-2 locus presents biological and computational characteristics that place it outside the scope of generic repeat genotyping tools. Its 5.6 kb repeat unit is orders of magnitude larger than the short tandem repeats (STRs, 1–6 bp motifs) targeted by most existing software. The VNTR region itself can span over 200 kb in individuals with high copy number, making read-pair spanning approaches infeasible with standard 150 bp paired-end reads. Critically, obtaining haplotype-resolved rather than diploid copy number estimates requires a phasing strategy that does not depend on long reads or prior genotyped variants, a requirement uniquely addressed by the IBD-based algorithm [12] implemented in GRiD. Beyond LPA, GRiD’s read-depth normalization and nearest-neighbor framework are locus-agnostic and applicable to any large VNTR; Mukamel et al. [11] identified 734 protein-coding VNTRs with elevated IBD rates across the human genome, all of which represent potential targets.

State of the Field¶

Several mature tools exist for genotyping tandem repeats from short-read sequencing data. ExpansionHunter [16] and GangSTR [17] use probabilistic models of read-pair orientation and coverage to estimate repeat lengths, but are designed for STRs and short VNTRs where reads can partially or fully span the repeat unit. HipSTR [18] performs haplotype-level STR genotyping using phased reads but is similarly constrained to short repeat units accessible by individual reads. Tools such as TRTools [19] provide downstream analysis utilities for STR calls but do not extend to large-VNTR loci. adVNTR [20] uses hidden Markov models to genotype VNTRs genome-wide and supports both Illumina and PacBio data, but is designed for VNTRs of up to a few hundred base pairs where reads can overlap the repeat boundary, which is qualitatively different from the 5.6 kb KIV-2 repeat unit. More recently, long-read platforms have enabled direct assembly of complex repeat regions [21], and Kivvi [14] specifically targets KIV-2 from PacBio HiFi data; however, long-read data are not yet available for most large epidemiological cohorts, which remain dominated by short-read WGS.

For the LPA KIV-2 locus specifically, KILDA [13] provides an alignment-free diploid copy number estimate from short-read FASTQ files using a k-mer counting strategy calibrated against long-read assemblies. While KILDA demonstrates strong concordance with assembly-based diploid estimates, it does not address haplotype-level phasing. The foundational algorithmic framework integrated by GRiD was established by Mukamel et al. [11], who demonstrated that population-normalized read depth is the most tractable approach for copy number estimation from short-read cohort data, and by Hujoel et al. [12], who developed an IBD-based iterative algorithm for decomposing diploid copy number into haplotype-specific contributions without requiring long reads or pre-existing phased variant data. GRiD integrates and operationalizes both methodological contributions into a single accessible software package, read depth profile-aware nearest-neighbor normalization, a full configuration system, and multi-cohort scalability.

Software Design¶

GRiD is implemented in Python (≥3.8) and follows a modular architecture organized into seven sequential pipeline steps, each implemented as an independent utility module under grid/utils/. The pipeline is orchestrated by grid/pipeline.py and driven by a user-supplied YAML configuration file, which specifies file paths, genomic coordinates, per-step parameters, and which steps to execute. The command-line interface is implemented with Click [22] and provides an entry point: grid wgs for whole-genome sequencing data.

The Whole Genome Sequencing pipeline proceeds as follows:

  1. Index verification / creation: Ensures CRAM/BAM index files (.crai/.bai) exist for all input samples, creating them with samtools [23] if needed.

  2. Read counting: Counts properly paired reads in the KIV-2 VNTR region using pysam [24], filtering by mapping quality and SAM flag.

  3. Coverage estimation: Runs mosdepth [25] across the genome in binned mode to produce per-sample depth profiles.

  4. Coverage normalization: Normalizes coverage within individuals (by sample mean depth) and across individuals (by a z-score transformation), then filters to high-variance regions enriched for VNTR signal.

  5. Nearest-neighbor identification: Uses scikit-learn [26] to compute Euclidean distances in normalized depth space and identify the top-N genomically similar neighbors per individual, providing a cohort-matched reference for CNV normalization.

  6. Diploid copy number estimation: Computes per-exon diploid copy number for each individual by normalizing their read counts relative to their nearest neighbors’ read counts, accounting for sample-specific sequencing depth.

  7. Haplotype inference: Applies an IBD-based iterative phasing algorithm [12] that uses haplotype-matched neighbors to decompose diploid copy number estimates into haplotype-specific contributions.

All multi-sample steps are parallelized using Python’s concurrent.futures.ThreadPoolExecutor, and progress is reported via the Rich terminal library [27]. Configuration validation at pipeline startup provides early, informative error messages before any compute-intensive steps run.

Research Impact Statement¶

GRiD has been validated across multiple large-scale, multi-ancestry whole-genome sequencing cohorts representing tens of thousands of individuals spanning Hispanic/Latino, African American, and European American ancestry groups. Its nearest-neighbor normalization approach naturally accounts for ancestry and sequencing batch effects without requiring explicit ancestry labels, making it well-suited for population-diverse studies where CNV tools calibrated on European-ancestry reference panels may systematically underperform.

GRiD has attracted national and international interest from research groups across multiple conferences, with inquiries from groups seeking to apply KIV-2 copy number estimation to their own short-read WGS cohorts. It is designed for accessibility, with comprehensive documentation of configuration, outputs, and algorithmic details to lower the barrier to entry for epidemiologists and clinicians without extensive bioinformatics training.

Mathematics¶

Coverage estimation. Weighted mean depth across the VNTR region from mosdepth bins is computed as:

\[ \text{Coverage} = \left\lfloor 100 \cdot \frac{ \sum_{i \in R} \left( \bar{C}_i \cdot \bigl( \min(e, r_{i,e}) - \max(s, r_{i,s}) \bigr) \right) }{ \sum_{i \in R} \bigl( \min(e, r_{i,e}) - \max(s, r_{i,s}) \bigr) } \right\rceil \]

where \(R\) is the set of bins overlapping the region \([s, e]\), \(\bar{C}_i\) is the mean depth of bin \(i\), and \(r_{i,s}\), \(r_{i,e}\) are the bin boundaries.

Normalization. Let \(D \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times M}\) be the depth matrix for \(N\) individuals across \(M\) genomic bins. Within-individual normalization gives \(D^{(1)}_{ij} = D_{ij} / \bar{D}_i\), where \(\bar{D}_i\) is the individual mean. Across-individual normalization then yields:

\[ D^{\mathrm{norm}}_{ij} = \frac{D^{(1)}_{ij} - \mu_j}{\sqrt{\mu_j}} \]

where \(\mu_j\) is the population mean of \(D^{(1)}_{\cdot j}\). Regions are filtered by a variance ratio \(\sigma_j^2 / \mu_j\), and the top fraction of high-variance regions are retained for neighbor computation.

Neighbor-normalized diploid copy number. For individual \(i\) with read count \(r_i\) and depth scale \(s_i\), the diploid copy number estimate is:

\[ \widehat{\text{dipCN}}_i = \frac{r_i / s_i}{\frac{1}{|N_i|} \sum_{j \in N_i} r_j / s_j} \]

where \(N_i\) is the set of nearest neighbors identified from the normalized depth space.

Haplotype inference. Following Hujoel et al. [12], haplotype-specific copy numbers \(h_{i,1}\) and \(h_{i,2}\) are iteratively estimated by updating each haplotype’s value proportionally to the mean copy number of IBD-matched haplotype neighbors:

\[ h_{i,k}^{(t+1)} = \widehat{\text{dipCN}}_i \cdot \frac{\bar{h}_{N_{i,k}}^{(t)}}{\bar{h}_{N_{i,1}}^{(t)} + \bar{h}_{N_{i,2}}^{(t)}} \]

where \(\bar{h}_{N_{i,k}}^{(t)}\) is the mean haplotype copy number of IBD neighbors on haplotype \(k\) at iteration \(t\).
